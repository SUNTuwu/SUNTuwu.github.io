If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then when computing $\int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x$ from definition, we may simplify the calculation by just choosing any convenient Riemann sums of $f$ (e.g., the right Riemann sum) with respect to $n$ subintervals of equal width $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$. Specifically, for right Riemann sum: $$ x_{1}=a+\Delta x, \quad x_{2}=a+2 \Delta x, \quad x_{3}=a+3 \Delta x, \quad \cdots \cdots $$ In general, $x_{i}=a+i \Delta x$. Definition of definite integral simply becomes: $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(a+i \Delta x) \Delta x . $$ On the interval $[a, b]=[0,1]: a=0, b=1, \Delta x=\frac{1-0}{n}=\frac{1}{n}$. So, this formula reduces further to $$ \int_{0}^{1} f(x) \mathrm{d} x=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(\frac{i}{n}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{n} . $$
