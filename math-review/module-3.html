How to find the length of a curve defined by $y=f(x)$ ? (1) Divide $[a, b]$ into $n$ intervals with equal length $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}, x_{i}=a+i \Delta x$, $i=0,1, \ldots, n$. (2) Line segment $P_{i-1} P_{i}$ is a good approximation of the curve segment $P_{i-1} P_{i}$ The length of the curve $C$ with equation $y=f(x)$ is $$ L=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|P_{i-1} P_{i}\right| $$ Note that $\left|P_{i-1} P_{i}\right|=\sqrt{\left(x_{i}-x_{i-1}\right)^{2}+\left(f\left(x_{i}\right)-f\left(x_{i-1}\right)\right)^{2}}$. By applying Mean Value Theorem on $\left[x_{i-1}, x_{i}\right]$, there is a number $x_{i}^{*}$ between $x_{i-1}$ and $x_{i}$ such that $$ f\left(x_{i}\right)-f\left(x_{i-1}\right)=f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\left(x_{i}-x_{i-1}\right) $$ By applying Mean Value Theorem on $\left[x_{i-1}, x_{i}\right]$, there is a number $x_{i}^{*}$ between $x_{i-1}$ and $x_{i}$ such that $$ \begin{aligned} f\left(x_{i}\right)-f & \left(x_{i-1}\right)=f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\left(x_{i}-x_{i-1}\right) \\ \Longrightarrow \quad\left|P_{i-1} P_{i}\right| & =\sqrt{(\Delta x)^{2}+\left[f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right) \Delta x\right]^{2}} \\ & =\sqrt{1+\left[f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\right]^{2}} \Delta x \end{aligned} $$ By applying Mean Value Theorem on $\left[x_{i-1}, x_{i}\right]$, there is a number $x_{i}^{*}$ between $x_{i-1}$ and $x_{i}$ such that $$ \begin{aligned} f\left(x_{i}\right)-f & \left(x_{i-1}\right)=f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\left(x_{i}-x_{i-1}\right) \\ \Longrightarrow \quad\left|P_{i-1} P_{i}\right| & =\sqrt{(\Delta x)^{2}+\left[f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right) \Delta x\right]^{2}} \\ & =\sqrt{1+\left[f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\right]^{2}} \Delta x \end{aligned} $$ ⟹ By the definition, $$ \begin{aligned} L=\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|P_{i-1} P_{i}\right| & =\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sqrt{1+\left[f^{\prime}\left(x_{i}^{*}\right)\right]^{2}} \Delta x \\ & =\int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{1+\left[f^{\prime}(x)\right]^{2}} \mathrm{~d} x \end{aligned} $$ Theorem (Arc Length Formula: $y=f(x)$ ) If $f^{\prime}$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then the length of the curve $y=f(x)$, $a \leq x \leq b$, is $$ L=\int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{1+\left[f^{\prime}(x)\right]^{2}} \mathrm{~d} x=\int_{a}^{b} \sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\mathrm{d} y}{\mathrm{~d} x}\right)^{2}} \mathrm{~d} x $$

(Hint: $\int \sec ^{3} \theta \mathrm{~d} \theta=\frac{1}{2}(\sec \theta \tan \theta+\ln |\sec \theta+\tan \theta|)+C$.) Remark 1 $$ \begin{aligned} \int \sec ^{3} \theta \mathrm{~d} \theta=\int \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \tan \theta & =\sec \theta \tan \theta-\int \tan \theta \mathrm{d} \sec \theta \\ & =\sec \theta \tan \theta-\int \tan \theta \cdot \tan \theta \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \theta \\ & =\sec \theta \tan \theta-\int\left(\sec ^{2} \theta-1\right) \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \theta \\ & =\sec \theta \tan \theta-\int \sec ^{3} \theta \mathrm{~d} \theta+\int \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \theta \end{aligned} $$ Hence, $\int \sec ^{3} \theta d \theta=\frac{1}{2}\left(\sec \theta \tan \theta+\int \sec \theta d \theta\right)$. On the other hand, $$ \begin{aligned} \int \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \theta & =\int \frac{\sec \theta(\sec \theta+\tan \theta)}{\sec \theta+\tan \theta} \mathrm{d} \theta \\ & =\int \frac{\mathrm{d}(\sec \theta+\tan \theta)}{\sec \theta+\tan \theta} \\ & =\ln |\sec \theta+\tan \theta|+C \end{aligned} $$ Hence, $\int \sec ^{3} \theta d \theta=\frac{1}{2}(\sec \theta \tan \theta+\ln |\sec \theta+\tan \theta|)+C$. answer: average-value limit
What is the average value of a function $y=f(x), a \leq x \leq b$ ? (1) Divide the interval $[a, b]$ into $n$ equal subintervals with length $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$. Moreover, we have $x_{i}=a+i \Delta x$ and choose $x_{i}^{*} \in\left[x_{i-1}, x_{i}\right]$. (2) The average of the numbers $f\left(x_{1}^{*}\right), f\left(x_{2}^{*}\right), \ldots, f\left(x_{n}^{*}\right)$ is $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{f\left(x_{1}^{*}\right)+f\left(x_{2}^{*}\right)+\cdots+f\left(x_{n}^{*}\right)}{n} & =\frac{f\left(x_{1}^{*}\right)+f\left(x_{2}^{*}\right)+\cdots+f\left(x_{n}^{*}\right)}{\frac{b-a}{\Delta x}} \\ & =\frac{1}{b-a}\left[f\left(x_{1}^{*}\right) \Delta x+f\left(x_{2}^{*}\right) \Delta x+\cdots+f\left(x_{n}^{*}\right) \Delta x\right] \\ & =\frac{1}{b-a} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(x_{i}^{*}\right) \Delta x \end{aligned} $$ (3) The limiting value is $\lim _{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{b-a} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(x_{i}^{*}\right) \Delta x=\frac{1}{b-a} \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x$. The average value of $f$ on the interval $[a, b]$ is defined as $$ f_{\mathrm{ave}}=\frac{1}{b-a} \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x $$

On the other hand, $$ \begin{aligned} \int \sec \theta \mathrm{d} \theta=\int \frac{\sec \theta(\sec \theta+\tan \theta)}{\sec \theta+\tan \theta} \mathrm{d} \theta & =\int \frac{\mathrm{d}(\sec \theta+\tan \theta)}{\sec \theta+\tan \theta} \\ & =\ln |\sec \theta+\tan \theta|+C \end{aligned} $$ Hence, $\int \sec ^{3} \theta \mathrm{~d} \theta=\frac{1}{2}(\sec \theta \tan \theta+\ln |\sec \theta+\tan \theta|)+C$. (2) Let $\alpha=\tan ^{-1} e$. Then $\tan \alpha=e$. Hence, $\sec ^{2} \alpha=1+\tan ^{2} \alpha=1+e^{2}$ and $\sec \alpha=\sqrt{1+e^{2}}$, i.e., $\sec \left(\tan ^{-1} e\right)=\sqrt{1+e^{2}}$.
