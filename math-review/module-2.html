Strategy for Evaluating $\int \tan ^{m} x \sec ^{n} x \mathrm{~d} x$ (a) If $n=2 k, k \in \mathbb{N}$, then save a factor of $\sec ^{2} x$ and use $\sec ^{2} x=1+\tan ^{2} x$ to express the remaining factors in terms of $\tan x$ : $$ \begin{aligned} \int \tan ^{m} x \sec ^{2 k} x \mathrm{~d} x & =\int \tan ^{m} x \sec ^{2(k-1)} x \sec ^{2} x \mathrm{~d} x \\ & =\int \tan ^{m} x \sec ^{2(k-1)} \mathrm{d} \tan x \end{aligned} $$ Then substitute $u=\tan x$. (b) If $m=2 k+1$ and $n \geq 1$, then save sec $x \tan x$ and use $\tan ^{2}=\sec ^{2}-1$ to express the remaining factors in terms of $\sec x$ : $$ \begin{aligned} \int \tan ^{2 k+1} x \sec ^{n} x \mathrm{~d} x & =\int\left(\tan ^{2} x\right)^{k} \sec ^{n-1} x \sec x \tan x \mathrm{~d} x \\ & =\int\left(\sec ^{2} x-1\right)^{k} \sec ^{n-1} x \mathrm{~d} \sec x \end{aligned} $$ Then substitute $u=\sec x$. Identities (I) $\int \tan x d x=\ln |\sec x|+C$ (II) $\int \sec x \mathrm{~d} x=\ln |\sec x+\tan x|+C$

definition-(partial-fraction) improper-integrals partial-fractions ratio-test
How does the method of partial fraction work in general? Consider a rational function $f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$, where $P$ and $Q$ are polynomials. If $P(x)=a_{n} x^{n}+a_{n-1} x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_{1} x+a_{0}\left(a_{n} \neq 0\right)$, then the degree of $P$ is $n$ and denoted by $\operatorname{deg}(P)=n$. If $\operatorname{deg}(P)<\operatorname{deg}(Q)$, we call $f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$ is proper; otherwise, it is improper. For a proper rational function, it is possible to express it as a sum of simpler fractions. - Step 1: If $f(x)$ is improper, perform the long division and get $$ f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}=S(x)+\frac{R(x)}{Q(x)} $$ - Step 2: Factor the denominator as far as possible - Step 3: Express the proper rational function $\frac{R(x)}{Q(x)}$ as a sum of partial fractions of the form $$ \frac{A}{(a x+b)^{i}} \text { or } \frac{A x+B}{\left(a x^{2}+b x+c\right)^{j}} . $$
