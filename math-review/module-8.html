Exercise Find a power series representation for $\frac{1}{x+2}$. Theorem (term-by-term differentiation and integration) If the power series $\sum c_{n}(x-a)^{n}$ has radius of convergence $R>0$, then the function $f$ defined by $$ f(x)=c_{0}+c_{1}(x-a)+c_{2}(x-a)^{2}+\cdots=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n}(x-a)^{n} $$ is differentiable (and therefore continuous) on the interval ( $a-R, a+R$ ) and (i) $$ \begin{aligned} f^{\prime}(x) & =\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{~d} x}\left[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n}(x-a)^{n}\right]=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{~d} x}\left[c_{n}(x-a)^{n}\right] \\ & =c_{1}+2 c_{2}(x-a)+3 c_{3}(x-a)^{2}+\cdots=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n c_{n}(x-a)^{n-1} \end{aligned} $$ (ii) $\int\left[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n}(x-a)^{n}\right] \mathrm{d} x=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \int c_{n}(x-a)^{n} \mathrm{~d} x=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{c_{n}}{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}+C$ The radii of convergence of the power series in Equations (i) and (ii) are both $R$. The radius of convergence remains the same when a power series is differentiated or integrated. However, the interval of convergence may be different.
