Module 1: Foundations of Integration

Lectures 1–2 · 3–4 hours
▶

Topics

1. Derivative Review

  • Derivative rules (power, product, quotient, chain rule)
  • Trigonometric derivatives
  • Derivative table (all basic functions)

2. Riemann Sums & the Definite Integral

  • Riemann sum definition (right/left/midpoint endpoints)
  • Definite integral as limit of Riemann sums
  • Properties of definite integrals (linearity, order reversal, zero-width, sum of intervals)

3. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)

  • FTC Part 1: derivative of an integral
  • FTC Part 2: evaluating definite integrals via antiderivatives

4. The Substitution Rule

  • U-substitution for indefinite integrals
  • Changing limits for definite integrals
  • Symmetry: integrating even/odd functions over symmetric intervals

5. Areas Between Curves

  • A = ∫_a^b [f(x) − g(x)] dx (top minus bottom)
  • Region bounded by intersecting curves
  • Handling y as function of x

6. Volumes

  • By cross-section: V = ∫_a^b A(x) dx
  • Solids of revolution:
  • Disk method: V = ∫ π [R(x)]² dx (rotation about x-axis)
  • Washer method: V = ∫ π ([R_out(x)]² − [R_in(x)]²) dx
  • Rotating about lines other than axes (y = c, x = c)
  • Rotating about the y-axis (y as variable)

Study Recommendations

  • High priority: FTC (both parts) and u-substitution are used throughout every later module
  • Common mistakes: Forgetting to change limits in definite u-sub; using wrong radius function in washer method; missing the π factor in volume formulas
  • Practice strategy: Before attempting any volume problem, draw the region and axis of rotation. Identify whether disks, washers, or cross-sections apply.

Module 2: Advanced Integration Techniques

Lectures 6–9 · 6–8 hours (largest and most technical module)
▶

Topics

1. Integration by Parts

  • Formula: ∫ u dv = uv − ∫ v du
  • Choosing u and dv (LIATE / ILATE priority)
  • Repeated integration by parts (cyclic integrals like ∫ e^x sin x dx)
  • Definite integrals with parts

2. Reduction Formulas

  • For ∫ sin^n x dx
  • Using reduction to lower powers iteratively

3. Trigonometric Integrals

  • Case 1 (sin^m cos^n):
  • If m odd: save sin, convert rest to cos, sub u = cos x
  • If n odd: save cos, convert rest to sin, sub u = sin x
  • If both even: use half-angle identities (sin²x = (1−cos 2x)/2, cos²x = (1+cos 2x)/2)
  • Product-to-sum identities for sin(mx) cos(nx), etc.
  • Case 2 (tan^m sec^n):
  • If n even: save sec², convert rest to tan, sub u = tan x
  • If m odd: save sec x tan x, convert rest to sec, sub u = sec x

4. Trigonometric Substitution

  • Three substitution patterns:
  • √(a² − x²) → x = a sin θ
  • √(a² + x²) → x = a tan θ
  • √(x² − a²) → x = a sec θ
  • Completing the square before substitution
  • Converting back to x using reference triangles

5. Partial Fractions

  • Case 1: Distinct linear factors
  • Case 2: Repeated linear factors
  • Case 3: Irreducible quadratic factors
  • Case 4: Repeated irreducible quadratic factors
  • General steps: (1) long division if degree num ≥ degree den; (2) factor denominator; (3) set up decomposition; (4) solve for coefficients

$∫ sin^n x dx = −1/n sin^{n−1} x cos x + (n−1)/n ∫ sin^{n−2} x dx$Reduction for sine
$x = a sin θ, dx = a cos θ dθ$Sub for √(a²−x²)
$x = a tan θ, dx = a sec² θ dθ$Sub for √(a²+x²)

Study Recommendations

  • High priority: Integration by parts and partial fractions appear most frequently in exam problems
  • Common mistakes: Forgetting the − sign in parts; wrong choice of u; missing the dx = a cos θ dθ in trig sub; not doing long division first in partial fractions
  • Practice strategy: For partial fractions, always check if numerator degree ≥ denominator degree first. For trig integrals, classify the form (sin/cos vs tan/sec) before choosing the method. For trig sub, draw the reference triangle every time.

Module 3: Applications of Integration

Lectures 4–5 · 2–3 hours
▶

Topics

1. Average Value of a Function

  • f_avg = (1/(b−a)) ∫_a^b f(x) dx
  • Geometric interpretation: rectangle with area equal to the integral

2. Mean Value Theorem for Integrals

  • If f continuous on [a,b], then ∃ c ∈ [a,b] such that f(c) = f_avg
  • Connection to MVT for derivatives

3. Arc Length

  • For y = f(x): L = ∫_a^b √(1 + [f'(x)]²) dx
  • For x = g(y): L = ∫_c^d √(1 + [g'(y)]²) dy
  • The arc length function: s(x) = ∫_a^x √(1 + [f'(t)]²) dt

4. Area of a Surface of Revolution

  • About x-axis (y = f(x)): S = ∫_a^b 2π y √(1 + [y']²) dx
  • About y-axis (y = f(x)): S = ∫_a^b 2π x √(1 + [y']²) dx
  • For x = g(y) about y-axis: S = ∫_c^d 2π y √(1 + [x']²) dy
  • Derivation: frustum of a cone (lateral surface area = 2πrl)
FormulaDescription
$f_avg = 1/(b−a) ∫_a^b f(x) dx$Average value
MVT: `∃ c: f(c) = f_avg`Mean Value Theorem for Integrals
$L = ∫ √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx$Arc length (y = f(x))
$L = ∫ √(1 + (dx/dy)²) dy$Arc length (x = g(y))

Study Recommendations

  • Medium priority: These topics are narrower but frequently appear as exam questions
  • Common mistakes: Confusing arc length formula with area formula; forgetting the 2πr factor in surface area; using wrong radius for rotation axis
  • Practice strategy: Arc length integrals often result in messy algebra — practice simplifying the integrand before integrating. Surface area is more common on exams than average value.

Module 4: Numerical Integration

Lectures 10–11 · 2–3 hours
▶

Topics

1. Strategy for Integration (4-Step Framework)

  • (1) Simplify (algebraic manipulation)
  • (2) Substitute (choose substitution)
  • (3) Classify (identify form: parts, trig, partial fractions, etc.)
  • (4) Try again (if stuck, revisit steps)
  • Reference: 16-formula table of integrals

2. Approximate Integration Rules

  • Left/Right Endpoint: L_n = Δx Σ f(x_{i−1}), R_n = Δx Σ f(x_i)
  • Midpoint Rule: M_n = Δx Σ f(x̄_i) where x̄_i is midpoint
  • Trapezoidal Rule: T_n = (Δx/2)[f(x₀)+2f(x₁)+2f(x₂)+⋯+f(x_n)]

3. Error Bounds

  • Trapezoidal: |E_T| ≤ K(b−a)³ / (12n²) where |f''(x)| ≤ K
  • Midpoint: |E_M| ≤ K(b−a)³ / (24n²)
  • Using error bounds to find minimum n for given tolerance

4. Simpson's Rule

  • S_n = (Δx/3)[f(x₀) + 4f(x₁) + 2f(x₂) + 4f(x₃) + ⋯ + f(x_n)]
  • n must be even
  • Error bound: |E_S| ≤ K(b−a)⁵ / (180n⁴) where |f⁽⁴⁾(x)| ≤ K

$S_n = Δx/3[f₀+4f₁+2f₂+4f₃+⋯+f_n]$Simpson's Rule (n even)

Study Recommendations

  • Medium priority: Numerical integration is less emphasized in pure calculus courses but essential for applications
  • Common mistakes: Using odd n for Simpson's Rule; forgetting the alternating 4-2-4-2 pattern; confusing error bound formulas
  • Practice strategy: For error bound problems, always take the absolute value of the max of the relevant derivative over the interval. Memorize which error formula uses which derivative order.

Module 5: Polar Coordinates & Parametric Curves

Lectures 12-1, 13-1 · 2–3 hours
▶

Topics

1. Polar Coordinates

  • Conversion: x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ; r² = x² + y², tan θ = y/x
  • Multiple representations: (r, θ) and (r, θ+2π); (−r, θ) = (r, θ+π)
  • Symmetry tests (x-axis, y-axis, origin)

2. Tangents to Polar Curves

  • dy/dx = ( (dr/dθ) sin θ + r cos θ ) / ( (dr/dθ) cos θ − r sin θ )
  • Horizontal tangents: numerator = 0, denominator ≠ 0
  • Vertical tangents: denominator = 0, numerator ≠ 0

3. Area in Polar Coordinates

  • A = ∫_α^β ½ [f(θ)]² dθ
  • Area between two polar curves: A = ∫_α^β ½ ([r_out]² − [r_in]²) dθ
  • Finding intersection points

4. Arc Length in Polar Coordinates

  • L = ∫_α^β √(r² + (dr/dθ)²) dθ

$x = r cos θ, y = r sin θ$Polar to Cartesian
$r² = x² + y², tan θ = y/x$Cartesian to polar
$dy/dx = (r' sin θ + r cos θ)/(r' cos θ − r sin θ)$Tangent slope in polar
$A = ∫ ½ r² dθ$Area in polar
$A = ∫ ½ (r_out² − r_in²) dθ$Area between polar curves

Study Recommendations

  • Medium-high priority: Polar area and arc length are common exam topics
  • Common mistakes: Forgetting the ½ in the area formula; not squaring r in arc length formula; using wrong limits of integration when finding intersection points
  • Practice strategy: For area between polar curves, graph both curves first to visualize which is outer. For tangents, the formula is derived from parametric differentiation — understand the derivation rather than just memorizing.

Module 6: Improper Integrals

Lecture 14-1 · 1.5–2 hours
▶

Topics

1. Type 1: Infinite Intervals

  • ∫_a^∞ f(x) dx = lim_{t→∞} ∫_a^t f(x) dx
  • ∫_{-∞}^b f(x) dx = lim_{t→−∞} ∫_t^b f(x) dx
  • ∫_{-∞}^∞ f(x) dx = ∫_{-∞}^c + ∫_c^∞ (both must converge)

2. Type 2: Discontinuous Integrands

  • If f has vertical asymptote at x = a: ∫_a^b f(x) dx = lim_{t→a⁺} ∫_t^b f(x) dx
  • If f has vertical asymptote at x = b: ∫_a^b f(x) dx = lim_{t→b⁻} ∫_a^t f(x) dx
  • If f has discontinuity at interior c: split at c

3. p-Integrals

  • ∫_1^∞ (1/x^p) dx: converges if p > 1, diverges if p ≤ 1
  • ∫_0^1 (1/x^p) dx: converges if p < 1, diverges if p ≥ 1

4. Comparison Theorem

  • If 0 ≤ f(x) ≤ g(x) on [a,∞):
  • If ∫ g converges, then ∫ f converges
  • If ∫ f diverges, then ∫ g diverges
  • Limit comparison test variant
FormulaDescription
$∫_a^∞ f(x) dx = lim_{t→∞} ∫_a^t f(x) dx$Type 1 definition
$∫_a^b f(x) dx = lim_{t→a⁺} ∫_t^b f(x) dx$Type 2 (asymptote at a)
`∫_1^∞ 1/x^p dx` conv p>1, div p≤1p-test for infinite
`∫_0^1 1/x^p dx` conv p<1, div p≥1p-test for finite

Study Recommendations

  • High priority: Improper integrals appear frequently as both standalone questions and within series convergence tests
  • Common mistakes: Not handling both limits independently for double-infinite integrals; forgetting to check for vertical asymptotes within the interval; using comparison theorem backwards
  • Practice strategy: Always identify the type (infinite limit vs. discontinuous integrand, or both) before setting up the limit. For comparison, find a simpler function that bounds yours.

Module 7: Infinite Sequences & Series

Lectures 15–18 · 5–7 hours
▶

Topics

1. Sequences

  • Definition, limit of a sequence
  • Limit laws (same as function limits)
  • Squeeze theorem for sequences
  • Monotonic sequences (increasing/decreasing)
  • Bounded sequences (bounded above/below)
  • Monotonic sequence theorem: bounded monotonic sequences converge

2. Infinite Series

  • Partial sums: S_n = Σ_{i=1}^n a_i
  • Convergence: lim_{n→∞} S_n = S (finite)
  • Geometric series: Σ ar^{n−1} converges to a/(1−r) if |r| < 1
  • Necessary condition: If Σ a_n converges, then lim a_n = 0
  • Test for divergence: If lim a_n ≠ 0, series diverges
  • Algebra of convergent series (linearity)

3. Integral Test & p-Series

  • If f is positive, continuous, decreasing on [1,∞), then Σ f(n) and ∫_1^∞ f(x) dx converge/diverge together
  • p-series: Σ 1/n^p converges if p > 1, diverges if p ≤ 1
  • Remainder estimate: R_n = S − S_n, bounds via integral

4. Comparison Tests

  • Direct Comparison: If 0 ≤ a_n ≤ b_n and Σ b_n converges, then Σ a_n converges
  • Limit Comparison: lim a_n/b_n = c > 0 ⇒ both converge or both diverge
  • Estimating sums via comparison

5. Alternating Series

  • Alternating series test: If b_n decreasing → 0, then Σ (−1)^{n−1} b_n converges
  • Alternating series estimation theorem: |R_n| ≤ b_{n+1}
  • Absolute vs. conditional convergence

6. Ratio & Root Tests

  • Ratio test: lim |a_{n+1}/a_n| = L; L < 1 ⇒ converges, L > 1 ⇒ diverges, L = 1 ⇒ inconclusive
  • Root test: lim ⁿ√|a_n| = L; same criteria
  • When to use each (factorials → ratio, nth powers → root)

Study Recommendations

  • High priority: This is the most content-heavy module and a major exam focus
  • Common mistakes: Applying divergence test incorrectly (lim a_n = 0 does NOT guarantee convergence); forgetting that p-series requires p > 1 for convergence; using ratio test when L=1 and not knowing what to do next
  • Practice strategy: Create a decision tree for series — first check p-series/geometric forms, then divergence test, then comparison, then alternating, then ratio/root. Practice identifying which test applies quickly.

Module 8: Power Series, Taylor Series & Applications

Lectures 19–22 · 5–7 hours
▶

Topics

1. Strategy for Testing Series (Decision Tree)

  • Quick checks: p-series, geometric, compare to known forms
  • Divergence test
  • Alternating series test
  • Ratio test (general purpose)
  • Root test (nth powers)
  • Integral test (last resort for decreasing positive terms)

2. Power Series

  • Definition: Σ c_n (x − a)^n
  • Radius of convergence R (via ratio or root test)
  • Interval of convergence (test endpoints separately)
  • Endpoint behavior: may converge at one, both, or neither endpoint

3. Power Series Representations

  • Geometric series: 1/(1−x) = Σ x^n for |x| < 1
  • Differentiation of power series (term-by-term)
  • Integration of power series (term-by-term)
  • Building series for: 1/(1+x²), 1/(1−x)², ln(1+x), arctan x

4. Taylor & Maclaurin Series

  • Taylor series at a: f(x) = Σ f^{(n)}(a)/n! (x−a)^n
  • Maclaurin series (a = 0)
  • Taylor polynomials: T_n(x) = Σ_{k=0}^n f^{(k)}(a)/k! (x−a)^k
  • Remainder: R_n(x) = f(x) − T_n(x)
  • Taylor's inequality: |R_n(x)| ≤ M/(n+1)! |x−a|^{n+1} where |f^{(n+1)}(u)| ≤ M
  • Table of important Maclaurin series (eˣ, sin x, cos x, 1/(1−x), ln(1+x), arctan x, etc.)

5. Binomial Series

  • (1+x)^k = Σ (k choose n) x^n for |x| < 1
  • Generalized binomial coefficients: (k choose n) = k(k−1)⋯(k−n+1)/n!

6. Operations with Power Series

  • Multiplication of power series
  • Division of power series
  • Applications: relativistic kinetic energy approximation

Study Recommendations

  • High priority: Taylor/Maclaurin series and power series are a major exam focus and a capstone topic
  • Common mistakes: Forgetting to test endpoints when finding interval of convergence (separately for each endpoint); applying Taylor's inequality with wrong derivative order; confusing Taylor series with Maclaurin series
  • Practice strategy: Memorize the 7 Maclaurin series in the table above. For finding power series, always start from a known series (usually geometric) and transform via substitution, differentiation, or integration. For Taylor series, compute derivatives systematically at the center point.
