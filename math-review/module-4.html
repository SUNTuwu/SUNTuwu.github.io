Can we integrate all continuous functions? Answer: No! The following integrals cannot be integrated in terms of elementary functions: $$ \begin{aligned} & \int e^{x^{2}} \mathrm{~d} x, \quad \int \frac{e^{x}}{x} \mathrm{~d} x, \quad \int \sin \left(x^{2}\right) \mathrm{d} x, \quad \int \cos \left(e^{x}\right) \mathrm{d} x \\ & \int \sqrt{x^{3}+1} \mathrm{~d} x, \quad \int \frac{1}{\ln x} \mathrm{~d} x, \quad \int \frac{\sin x}{x} \mathrm{~d} x \end{aligned} $$ In fact, the majority of elementary functions don't have elementary antiderivatives.

When should be use Approximate Integration? (a) It is difficult, or even impossible, to find an antiderivative, e.g., $\int e^{x^{2}} d x$. (b) The function is determined from a scientific experiment through instrument readings or collected data, i.e., no formula for the function. - The definite integral is defined as a limit of Riemann sums, so any Riemann sum could be used as an approximation to the integral. - Divide $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals of equal length $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$. Then $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(x_{i}^{*}\right) \Delta x, $$ where $x_{i}^{*} \in\left[x_{i-1}, x_{i}\right]$. (a) Left end approximation: choose $x_{i}^{*}=x_{i-1}$ $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x \approx L_{n}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(x_{i-1}\right) \Delta x $$ (b) Right end approximation: choose $x_{i}^{*}=x_{i}$ $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x \approx R_{n}=\sum_{i=1}^{n} f\left(x_{i}\right) \Delta x $$ (c) Midpoint Rule: choose $x_{i}^{*}=\bar{x}_{i}=\frac{x_{i-1}+x_{i}}{2}$ $$ \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x \approx M_{n}=\Delta x\left[f\left(\bar{x}_{1}\right)+f\left(\bar{x}_{2}\right)+\cdots+f\left(\bar{x}_{n}\right)\right] $$ (d) Trapezoidal Rule: $\frac{L_{n}+R_{n}}{2}$ $$ \begin{aligned} & \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \mathrm{d} x \approx T_{n} \\ = & \frac{\Delta x}{2}\left[f\left(x_{0}\right)+2 f\left(x_{1}\right)+2 f\left(x_{2}\right)+\cdots+2 f\left(x_{n-1}\right)+f\left(x_{n}\right)\right] \end{aligned} $$ where $\Delta x=\frac{b-a}{n}$ and $x_{i}=a+i \Delta x$.
